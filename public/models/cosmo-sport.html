Overview

The Mazda Cosmo Sport 110S was Mazda's bold statement to the world that the rotary engine was ready for the road. Named after the vastness of space, the Cosmo Sport embodied the futuristic promise of Felix Wankel's revolutionary engine design.

With its sleek, aerodynamic body designed by Matasaburo Maeda and Heiji Ikeda, the Cosmo Sport looked like nothing else on the road. Its low nose, flowing lines, and distinctive front grille made it an instant icon of 1960s automotive design.

Series I vs Series II

Series I (1967-1968)

The original Cosmo Sport featured a 110 hp 10A engine with carburetors. Only 343 units were produced, making it extremely rare today. Key identifiers include the smaller taillights and slightly different front grille.

Series II (1968-1972)

The updated Series II received a more powerful 128 hp engine, larger taillights, and a 5-speed manual transmission. Power steering became an option. The majority of Cosmo Sports produced were Series II models.

Design

The Cosmo Sport's design was inspired by contemporary jet aircraft. Its low nose allowed for excellent aerodynamics, while the long hood accommodated the compact rotary engine. The interior featured a futuristic dashboard with comprehensive gauges and a sporty three-spoke steering wheel.

Legacy

The Cosmo Sport established Mazda as a serious sports car manufacturer and proved the rotary engine could power a production vehicle. Its success paved the way for all future Mazda rotary models. Today, surviving Cosmo Sports are highly prized collector's items, with values continuing to rise.
