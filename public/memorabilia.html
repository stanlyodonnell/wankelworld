Rotary Memorabilia

Collectibles for the rotary enthusiast. From die-cast models to rare literature, celebrate your passion with tangible pieces of rotary history.

Die-Cast Models

Die-cast models offer a way to own every generation of rotary-powered Mazda without needing a bigger garage. From budget-friendly 1:64 scale to museum-quality 1:18 pieces, there's something for every collector.

Popular Scales

  • 1:64 Scale: Hot Wheels, Tomica, and Matchbox offer affordable RX-7s and RX-8s. Easy to collect the whole lineup without breaking the bank.
  • 1:43 Scale: The most popular scale for serious collectors. Brands like Ebbro, Kyosho, and AUTOart offer excellent detail at reasonable prices.
  • 1:18 Scale: The ultimate display pieces. AUTOart, Kyosho, and Ottomobile create stunning large-scale models with opening doors, hoods, and detailed engines.

Most Sought-After Models

  • 1970 Mazda Savanna RX-3: Racing liveries from the Japanese Grand Prix are particularly collectible
  • 1991 Mazda 787B: The Le Mans winner in its iconic orange and green Renown livery
  • FD RX-7: Especially in rare colors like Montego Blue and Vintage Red
  • Cosmo Sport 110S: The original—hard to find but worth the hunt
  • RX-8 Spirit R: The final rotary model, increasingly collectible

Resin Model Kits

Resin kits offer subjects that mass-market manufacturers won't touch. Garage kit makers produce limited runs of rare rotary models, from the Mazda 110S Cosmo Sport to the 787B Le Mans winner.

What to Know

Resin kits require assembly and painting. They're not toys—they're buildable art. Most come with photo-etched metal details, decals, and instructions. Expect to spend 20-40 hours on a quality build.

Notable Manufacturers

  • Hobby Design: High-quality resin kits of Japanese sports cars
  • Alpha Model: Excellent detail and fit
  • Studio27: Racing-focused subjects including the 787B
  • Hobby Fan: Good balance of detail and price

Building Tips

  • Wear a respirator when sanding resin—it's not good for your lungs
  • Wash parts before painting to remove mold release agents
  • Use CA glue for assembly—it bonds resin better than plastic cement
  • Prime before painting—resin can be porous

Literature & Documentation

Brochures, workshop manuals, and period magazines offer a window into how Mazda marketed and supported these unique cars. They're also invaluable for restoration projects.

Workshop Manuals

Original workshop manuals are essential for anyone maintaining a classic rotary. They're increasingly hard to find but worth tracking down. PDF versions are available online for many models.

Sales Brochures

Mazda's marketing materials showcase the rotary's evolution. Early brochures emphasized the technology; later ones focused on performance and driving experience. The FD RX-7 brochures are particularly beautiful.

Magazines

Period reviews offer contemporary perspectives on these cars. Look for:

  • Road & Track, Car and Driver: US magazine reviews
  • Motor Trend: Often featured RX-7 comparisons
  • Japanese magazines: Best Car, Option, and Hyper Rev for JDM content

Books

  • "Mazda RX-7" by Brian Long: Comprehensive history
  • "Rotary Engine" by Kenichi Yamamoto: By the father of Mazda's rotary program
  • "Wankel Rotary Engine" by Jan P. Norbye: Technical history

Apparel & Accessories

Wear your rotary pride. From subtle nods to full enthusiast gear, there's something for every level of devotion.

Clothing

  • Period-correct items: Vintage Mazda dealership shirts and jackets
  • Modern enthusiast gear: Rotary-themed t-shirts, hoodies, and caps
  • Racing team replicas: 787B Le Mans team wear

Accessories

  • Keychains featuring rotor housings
  • Lapel pins and badges
  • Posters and art prints
  • Mugs and drinkware

Engine Parts as Art

Old rotary parts can become display pieces. A rotor on a stand, a polished housing as a coffee table base, or apex seals mounted in a frame—these are conversation starters for your garage or office.

Popular Display Items

  • Rotor: The most recognizable rotary component. Clean it up and mount it on a stand.
  • Housing: A single rotor housing makes an interesting paperweight or display piece.
  • Eccentric shaft: When polished, these are beautiful mechanical sculptures.
  • Apex seals: Small but significant. A collection of seals from different engines shows evolution.

Where to Find Parts

  • eBay—always something listed
  • Rotary specialists sometimes sell display-quality parts
  • Engine rebuilds—ask your builder for a damaged rotor housing before it's scrapped
  • Junkyards—surprisingly good condition parts can be found

Collecting Tips

Start Small

Don't try to collect everything at once. Pick a focus: maybe just RX-7s, or just 1:43 scale, or just Le Mans-related items. A focused collection is more satisfying than a scattered one.

Condition Matters

For die-cast models, look for intact paint, no missing parts, and original packaging if possible. For literature, check for tears, stains, and writing. But don't let perfect be the enemy of good—rare items in less-than-perfect condition are still worth having.

Storage

  • Keep models out of direct sunlight—UV fades paint
  • Control humidity—metal parts can corrode
  • Display cases protect from dust and accidents
  • Store literature flat in acid-free sleeves

Authentication

The collectibles market has fakes. Buy from reputable dealers. For high-value items, ask for provenance. If a deal seems too good to be true, it probably is.

Community

Join collecting forums and Facebook groups. Other collectors are the best source for rare items, trade opportunities, and knowledge. The rotary community is generous with information.
