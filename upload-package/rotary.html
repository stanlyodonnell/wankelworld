Rotary Engine History

Understanding the genius of the Wankel rotary engine: how it works, its evolution, and why it remains one of automotive's most fascinating technologies.

How the Rotary Engine Works

Unlike a conventional piston engine, the Wankel rotary engine uses a triangular rotor that spins inside an epitrochoid-shaped housing. This design eliminates the reciprocating motion of pistons, creating a smooth, high-revving powerplant with an incredible power-to-weight ratio.

The Four Strokes

As the rotor turns, each of its three faces goes through the four cycles of intake, compression, combustion, and exhaust simultaneously. This means the rotary completes a power cycle every revolution of the eccentric shaft—compared to every two revolutions in a four-stroke piston engine.

Key Components

  • Rotor: The triangular component that spins eccentrically, sealed by apex seals at each corner
  • Housing: The epitrochoid-shaped chamber where combustion occurs
  • Eccentric Shaft: Similar to a crankshaft, converts rotor motion to output power
  • Apex Seals: Critical seals at each corner of the rotor that maintain compression
  • Side Housings: Contain the intake and exhaust ports

Advantages of the Rotary Engine

Compact and Light

A rotary engine produces similar power to a piston engine twice its displacement while weighing significantly less. A 13B rotary is roughly the size of a briefcase and can be lifted by one person.

Smooth Operation

With no reciprocating parts, the rotary has minimal vibration. It can spin to 9,000+ RPM with the smoothness of an electric motor.

High Revving

The rotary's simple rotating motion allows incredibly high redlines. Racing rotaries have exceeded 10,000 RPM, with the R26B in the 787B reaching over 9,000 RPM in endurance racing.

Simple Design

With only three moving parts in a twin-rotor engine (two rotors and the eccentric shaft), the rotary has fewer components to wear out compared to a piston engine with dozens of reciprocating parts.

Challenges and Solutions

Apex Seal Wear

The apex seals at each corner of the rotor are critical for compression. Early engines suffered from seal wear, but Mazda continuously improved seal materials, culminating in the Renesis engine with improved seal design.

Fuel Consumption

The rotary's combustion chamber shape and unburned fuel in the trailing pocket traditionally led to higher fuel consumption than piston engines. Mazda addressed this with direct injection in later concepts.

Oil Consumption

Rotary engines inject oil into the combustion chamber to lubricate the apex seals. While this causes some oil consumption, it's a necessary design feature for seal longevity.

Emissions

The combustion chamber's long, narrow shape made complete combustion challenging. Mazda solved this with improved port design, optimized ignition timing, and in later concepts, direct fuel injection.

10A Engine (1967-1973)

Mazda's first production rotary engine, the 10A powered the Cosmo Sport and R100. With a displacement of 982cc (491cc × 2 rotors), it produced 110-128 horsepower.

Key Features

  • Side intake ports with peripheral exhaust ports
  • Aluminum alloy housings with chrome plating
  • First production application of the rotary engine

12A Engine (1970-1985)

The 12A increased displacement to 1,146cc (573cc × 2 rotors) through thicker rotors. It powered the RX-2, RX-3, early RX-7, and the RX-5 Cosmo AP.

Key Features

  • Thicker rotors than the 10A for increased displacement
  • Thermal reactor for emissions control in later versions
  • First turbocharged rotary (in the Luce Legato)

13B Engine (1973-2012)

The most-produced rotary engine in history, the 13B displaced 1,308cc (654cc × 2 rotors). It powered everything from the RX-4 to the RX-8 and was produced for nearly 40 years.

Key Variants

  • 13B-AP: First 13B with side exhaust ports
  • 13B-T: Turbocharged variant (1986-1988)
  • 13B-Turbo II: Improved turbo with intercooler (1986-1991)
  • 13B-REW: Sequential twin-turbo (1992-2002)
  • 13B-MSP (Renesis): Side exhaust ports for improved emissions (2003-2012)

20B Engine (1990-1996)

The only production three-rotor engine from Mazda, the 20B displaced 1,962cc (654cc × 3 rotors) and produced up to 280 horsepower in the Eunos Cosmo.

Key Features

  • Three-rotor design for smooth, powerful operation
  • Sequential twin-turbocharging
  • Most powerful production rotary until the Renesis
  • Exclusive to the Eunos Cosmo luxury coupe

Renesis (2003-2012)

The final evolution of the 13B, the Renesis (13B-MSP) represented a fundamental rethinking of rotary design. By moving the exhaust ports to the side housings, Mazda improved emissions, fuel economy, and power.

Key Innovations

  • Side Exhaust Ports: Eliminated overlap between intake and exhaust for better emissions
  • 6-Port Design: Higher-revving version with additional intake ports
  • Improved Seals: Better apex seal design for longevity
  • Higher Compression: 10.0:1 compression ratio

Specifications

  • Displacement: 1,308cc (654cc × 2 rotors)
  • Power: 189-238 hp depending on configuration
  • Redline: 9,000 RPM
  • Applications: Mazda RX-8 (2003-2012)

R26B Racing Engine (1991)

The legendary R26B powered the Mazda 787B to victory at the 24 Hours of Le Mans—the only Japanese manufacturer and only rotary-powered car to win overall.

Specifications

  • Configuration: 4-rotor
  • Displacement: 2,616cc (654cc × 4 rotors)
  • Power: 700+ hp (racing trim)
  • Rev Limit: 9,000+ RPM

Le Mans Victory

In 1991, the Mazda 787B with the R26B engine won the 24 Hours of Le Mans, driven by Volker Weidler, Johnny Herbert, and Bertrand Gachot. The car's distinctive orange and green Renown livery and screaming rotary sound made it an instant legend.
