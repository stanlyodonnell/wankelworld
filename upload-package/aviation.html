Rotary Engines in Aviation

The Wankel rotary's compact size, light weight, and smooth operation make it ideal for aircraft. From early experiments to modern UAVs, the rotary has found its place in the sky.

Why Rotary Engines for Aircraft?

The Wankel rotary engine has several characteristics that make it particularly well-suited for aviation applications:

Power-to-Weight Ratio

Aviation is obsessed with weight. Every kilogram matters. The rotary produces similar power to a piston engine of twice the displacement while weighing significantly less. This means more payload or fuel capacity for the same engine weight.

Smooth Operation

The rotary's spinning motion produces minimal vibration. This reduces airframe fatigue, improves pilot comfort, and means avionics last longer without the constant shaking of a reciprocating engine.

Compact Size

The rotary's small physical dimensions allow for more aerodynamic cowling designs. A 13B rotary can fit where larger piston engines cannot, opening up design possibilities for aircraft manufacturers.

Reliability

With only three moving parts in a twin-rotor engine, there are fewer components to fail. No valves, valve springs, camshafts, or timing chains means less maintenance and fewer failure points.

Curtiss-Wright: The Aviation Pioneer

Curtiss-Wright was one of the first companies to license the Wankel engine for aviation. In the 1960s and 70s, they developed several rotary engines for aircraft use, including the RC2-60.

The RC2-60

  • Configuration: Twin-rotor
  • Displacement: 1.2L
  • Power: 185 hp
  • Weight: 182 lbs (dry)

The RC2-60 was used experimentally in various light aircraft and demonstrated the rotary's potential for aviation. Its power-to-weight ratio exceeded comparable piston engines of the era.

Cicaré Helicopters

Argentinian helicopter manufacturer Cicaré has used Wankel rotary engines in their light helicopters. The smooth power delivery and light weight of the rotary make it ideal for the demanding requirements of rotary-wing flight.

Notable Models

  • Cicaré CH-14: Experimental helicopter using a Mazda 13B engine
  • Cicaré CK.1: Ultralight helicopter with rotary power

The absence of vibration from the rotary engine is particularly valuable in helicopters, where vibration is a constant challenge for airframe and pilot fatigue.

Diamond Aircraft DA36 E-Star

In 2013, Diamond Aircraft unveiled the DA36 E-Star 2, a hybrid electric aircraft using a Wankel rotary engine as a range extender. This innovative application showcased the rotary's continued relevance in modern aviation.

How It Works

The rotary engine runs at constant RPM, driving a generator that powers electric motors connected to the propeller. This arrangement allows the engine to always run at its most efficient speed, dramatically improving fuel economy.

Benefits

  • 25% reduction in fuel consumption vs. conventional aircraft engines
  • Quieter operation
  • Reduced emissions
  • Simpler maintenance

Lockheed Martin Cypher UAV

The Cypher was an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) developed by Lockheed Martin in the 1990s. It used a Mazda 13B rotary engine and was notable for its unique ducted-fan design.

Specifications

  • Diameter: 1.14 meters (3.75 feet)
  • Height: 0.56 meters (1.8 feet)
  • Weight: 40 kg (88 lbs)
  • Engine: Mazda 13B rotary
  • Speed: Up to 100 km/h

The Cypher could hover and fly at low speeds, making it ideal for reconnaissance in urban environments. Its ducted-fan design was safer than exposed rotors and quieter than conventional helicopter designs.

Modern Applications

Today, the Wankel rotary continues to find new aviation applications, particularly in drones and UAVs where its characteristics are highly valued.

Drone Engines

Several companies produce Wankel engines specifically for drones:

  • UAV Engines Ltd (UEL): Produces the AR801 rotary engine specifically for UAVs
  • Austro Engine: Developed the AE50R rotary for light aircraft
  • Orbital Power: Creates rotary powerplants for military drones

Advantages for UAVs

  • Light weight allows longer flight times
  • Smooth operation protects sensitive payload equipment
  • Compact size fits in streamlined airframes
  • Reliability reduces mission failure risk

The Future

As electric aircraft development accelerates, the rotary engine is finding new life as a range extender. Its ability to run efficiently at constant RPM makes it ideal for generating electricity to supplement battery power. Several manufacturers are developing rotary-powered hybrid aircraft for both manned and unmanned applications.
