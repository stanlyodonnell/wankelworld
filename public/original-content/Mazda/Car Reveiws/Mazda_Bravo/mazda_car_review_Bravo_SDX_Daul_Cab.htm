    WORK utilities have come a long way in the last ten years. Mazda kicked off, in their "B" series of utilities with no frills work horses that were intended to do a job in reasonable comfort and style. Yet, today, those same vehicles have now progressed to what can only be regarded as a quite up market off roader in the form of the top of the range SDX Bravo dual cab utility.

    The heart of the Bravo series utility is the very smooth, quiet, twelve valve four cylinder two point six litre unleaded petrol engine (which powered my test vehicle) or, as an alternative, the two point five litre diesel power plant.

I found the unleaded petrol engine was so smooth that it could barely be heard running and yet that docile power plant fairly thrived on revs. Sure, it would snooze along in top gear at two thousand RPM but to make it really sing and pull strongly it was just a matter of getting a few more revs per minute into the equation. Once the tachometer reached the three thousand mark the engine was really working happily and starting to feel as though it would just keep right on going. Which it could, of course, as the red line was in the vicinity of the five thousand RPM mark.

The gear ratios in the five speed box were quite well matched to the engine and in low range four wheel drive the free revving engine kept the car going in loose stuff without much fuss or bother. A feature of the SDX specification is the automatic free wheeling hubs that are activated via a dash mounted switch, a useful feature if one mistakenly moves into soft going and four wheel drive is suddenly required to extract the car. A press of the button, a snappy gear selection from "2H to 4H" and that's all it takes to get all four wheels pulling their weight thanks to the combination of four wheel drive and the standard limited slip diff.

I found that a fair bag full of goodies are part and parcel of the Bravo's SDX specs. Starting from the front there is a special chrome treatment for the grille. Electrically operated mirrors and windows are part of the package, as is central locking. Velour trims on seat and doors, integrated door pockets, factory air conditioning, a chromed bull bar and last but not least the colour coded rear canopy completes the picture.

To my mind this vehicle is made for packing up and heading bush or up the beach. With four passengers plus driver seated within the cab there is a very large luggage space in the rear thanks to the huge lockable canopy area. I found the rear area large enough to sleep comfortably in, as a matter of fact. Scott and I spent a weekend camped out bush and all we did was to throw an old mattress in the rear section, plus some cooking gear and a bag of clothes and there we were, snug as two bugs in a rug.

Good sized fully screened windows are part of the package and we just slid them open to allow good ventilation and to keep the hungry buzzing biting hordes out where they belonged. To all intents and purpose the rear canopy is quite weather proof and it sure as hell is mosquito proof as well. That's for sure.

In the city or on the open highway the Mazda hums along quite well at the speed limit. Although the gear box ratios are sensibly spaced to make the engine work without over working it I felt that fifth gear tends to be fairly tall and if some fast overtaking needed to be done the smart move was to shift back to fourth and things will start to happen pretty quickly as the engine picks up revolutions.

In all the ute rides quite well although I somehow felt that the suspension package on this Bravo SDX tends to be just a tad too firm when the vehicle is unloaded. It's not rough, mind, more like a touch too willing to return to the position it was in before the bump, pot hole, or corrugation that was struck came along. Most likely I see this as a legacy of the Bravo series of utilities being primarily designed as work horses, for which Mazda makes no apologies. These vehicles are made to carry a solid payload and that's that.

On bad corrugations, however, the Bravo shows a tendency to bump steer somewhat although all it takes to correct the situation is to ease up a little on the loud pedal and the vehicle becomes as docile as a pussy cat again. In the paddocks, or sneaking down badly washed out tracks the Bravo remained easy to handle and the combination of a good driving position plus positive power steering all ensured that I had it pretty easy.

I see this vehicle as being pretty well the ideal size for a family man that requires a City vehicle for the week and a good recreation vehicle for weekends away.
